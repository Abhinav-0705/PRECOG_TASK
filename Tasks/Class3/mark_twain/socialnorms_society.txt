1. The Absurdity of the Hat Civilization has decreed that a man’s head must be covered at all times outdoors, and
uncovered at all times indoors, regardless of the temperature. A man meets a lady on the street in a freezing
gale. Etiquette demands he lift his hat, exposing his bald spot to the elements. He catches a cold; he dies.
But he died a gentleman. Conversely, in a drafty church, he must sit bareheaded while the heat escapes through his
skull, simply because it is considered disrespectful to the rafters to keep warm. The boy watches this ritual of the
hat—the lifting, the tipping, the waving—and concludes that it is a complex semaphore system designed chiefly to wear out the
brim and keep the hatters in business.

2. The weapon of the Fork The fork is a dangerous instrument introduced by society to test a man’s nerves. Nature
gave us fingers, and necessity gave us the spoon and the knife. The knife is a reliable tool; it carries
the beans to the mouth with safety and dispatch. But "Manners" insist that we balance round peas on the tines
of a silver fork. It is like trying to eat soup with a knitting needle. The boy watches the guests
at a dinner party, all terrified of dropping a morsel, all pretending that stabbing their food is a natural act.
He reckons that if we were truly civilized, we would eat with a shovel and get it over with, but
society prefers a method that ensures everyone leaves the table hungry and anxious.

3. The Ritual of the Calling Card There is a game played by adults called "Calling." It involves dressing up in
uncomfortable clothes and walking to a neighbor’s house to leave a small square of cardboard. The hope, secretly shared by
both parties, is that the neighbor is not home. If the neighbor is out, the caller leaves the card and
sighs with relief, having satisfied the social law without the burden of conversation. If the neighbor is home, both parties
must sit in the parlor for fifteen minutes, talking about the weather and hating every second of it. It is
a system of interaction based entirely on the mutual desire to avoid one another while pretending to be friends.

4. The Mystery of "Small Talk" When two people meet who have nothing to say to each other, they engage in
"Small Talk." This is a verbal vapor that fills the air but contains no substance. They discuss the rain, though
both can see it is raining. They discuss the heat, though both are sweating. To say what is actually on
one’s mind—"Your house smells like cabbage" or "I owe the bank fifty dollars"—is considered a high crime. The boy listens
to his parents chatter for an hour with the neighbors and realizes that they have exchanged thousands of words without
communicating a single idea. He decides that silence is not only golden, it is honest, which is why it is
so unpopular in the parlor.

5. The Despotism of the Guest Room Every house of pretension maintains a "Guest Room." It is the best room in
the house. The bed is high and soft; the pitcher is always full; the towels are snowy white. And it
stands empty for 360 days a year. The family crowds into small, drafty back rooms, sleeping on corn-shuck mattresses, while
the Guest Room collects dust. It is a shrine built to the hypothetical visitor. When a guest finally does arrive—usually
a distant aunt with a complaint—she is treated like royalty, while the master of the house sleeps in the barn.
The boy wonders why the family chooses to be miserable for the sake of a room they are forbidden to
enjoy.

6. The Fiction of the "Cut" Society has a weapon more deadly than a pistol: the "Cut." If a lady wishes
to destroy an enemy, she does not challenge her to a duel; she simply looks through her as if she
were made of glass. They meet on the street. The enemy smiles. The lady stares at a point three inches
above the enemy's bonnet and walks on. It is a public execution. The town gasps. The victim is socially dead.
The boy watches this invisible violence and thinks it is a cowardly way to fight. He prefers a punch in
the nose, which hurts for a minute but at least acknowledges that you exist, whereas the Cut erases you from
the book of life.

7. The Slavery of Fashion A woman does not dress for herself; she dresses for other women. If the fashion in
Paris dictates that skirts shall drag in the mud, every woman in town drags her skirt in the mud, bringing
home a collection of geological specimens on the hem. If the fashion says "bustles," she ties a pillow to her
back and looks like a camel. She suffers corsets that squeeze her liver into her throat. She claims to be
a free agent, yet she obeys the dictates of a tailor she has never met. The boy observes that while
men are slaves to their habits, women are slaves to the calendar, and that a hat that was beautiful in
June becomes a monstrosity by October.

8. The Hypocrisy of the Funeral A funeral is the community’s favorite entertainment. People who ignored the deceased for twenty years
suddenly discover a deep affection for him. They crowd the house to "view the remains." They weep loudly, checking to
see who is watching. They eat the widow’s ham with relish. It is a social event disguised as a tragedy.
The boy notices that the more a man was disliked in life, the larger the crowd at his burial, mostly
to make sure he is actually dead. He reckons that if the corpse could sit up and see who was
mourning him, he would laugh himself back to death at the absurdity of it all.

9. The Tyranny of the "Best China" Deep in the cupboard lies the "Best China." It is painted with delicate flowers
and is so thin you can almost see through it. It is never used. It is kept for an occasion
that is always in the future—a visit from the Bishop, perhaps, or the Second Coming. The family eats off chipped
earthenware every day, while the beautiful plates sit in the dark. The boy is terrified of the Best China. He
knows that to break a common plate is a misdemeanor, but to crack a teacup from the Best Set is
a felony punishable by exile. He learns that the most valuable things we own are the ones we are too
afraid to touch.

10. The Public Opinion There is a monster that lives in the town, invisible and sleepless. Its name is "They." "What
will They say?" the mother asks in a panic. "We cannot do that, or They will talk." No one knows
who They are. It is not the Sheriff; it is not the Parson. It is a collective ghost that polices
the curtains and the clotheslines. It forces people to wash their porches when they are tired and attend parties they
despise. The boy looks for They behind the bushes and around corners, but he never finds them. He only feels
their heavy hand, crushing the individuality out of the village until everyone looks and acts exactly like everyone else.

11. The Torture of the Parlor Piano It is a social law that every house of a certain standing must contain
a piano, and that the daughter of the house must be forced to assault it daily. The instrument usually sits
in the parlor, grinning with its ivory teeth, waiting for a victim. The girl, who has no more ear for
music than a cow has for astronomy, is made to hammer out "The Maiden’s Prayer" until the neighbors contemplate moving
to the next county. She hates it; the listeners hate it; the dog howls in protest. Yet, the performance continues.
It is not done for the love of melody, but for the sake of the reputation. To have a daughter
who cannot play is a confession of poverty or neglect, so the neighborhood must suffer the noise to prove that
the family has "Culture."

12. The Hypocrisy of the "Thank You" Note There is no document more deceitful than the letter of thanks written by
a boy after Christmas. He sits at the table, a prisoner of gratitude, with a pen that feels like a
crowbar. He has received a pair of wool socks and a book of sermons from his aunt. He wanted a
pistol. Yet, society demands that he write a letter expressing his boundless delight. He writes, "Dear Aunt, the socks are
just what I needed," while his soul blackens with the lie. He learns early that "manners" consist largely of saying
things you do not mean to people you do not like, and that the penalty for honesty is a reputation
for being an ungrateful savage.

13. The Martyrdom of the Stiff Collar On the Sabbath, a man is expected to encase his neck in a ring
of starched linen that is sharp enough to slice cheese. This is the Stiff Collar. It serves no purpose other
than to remind the wearer that he is miserable. It prevents him from looking left or right; it saws at
his jugular vein; it leaves a red welt on his skin. A man in a soft shirt is comfortable, and
therefore suspect. A man in a stiff collar is in agony, and therefore respectable. The boy watches the Deacons sitting
in their pews, red-faced and gasping like fish on a bank, and realizes that in our civilization, physical suffering is
the highest form of dignity.

14. The Venom of the Sewing Circle The Sewing Circle is an institution where women gather under the banner of charity
to construct garments for the heathen. This is the stated purpose. The actual purpose is the systematic destruction of local
reputations. As the needles fly, so do the rumors. "Have you noticed how Mrs. Miller walks?" "I hear the Baker
puts sawdust in his bread." The tongues work faster than the fingers. A reputation that took forty years to build
can be dismantled in the time it takes to hem a handkerchief. The boy observes that while the heathen may
eventually get a shirt, the neighbors are left with their characters shredded to ribbons, proving that charity acts as a
fine shield for malice.

15. The Tyranny of the "Rich Relation" Every family possesses one Relative who has more money than the rest combined. This
individual is usually cross, opinionated, and dull. Yet, when he visits, the household is turned upside down to please him.
The father laughs at his jokes, which are not funny. The mother cooks his favorite pudding, which gives everyone else
colic. The children are warned not to breathe loudly. He is treated with the reverence due to a visiting deity.
The boy knows that this affection is a sham; they are not loving the man, they are loving his Last
Will and Testament. They endure his presence today in the hope of inheriting his furniture tomorrow.

16. The Comedy of the Introduction When two strangers are thrown together, society insists on a ritual called the Introduction. A
third party mumbles two names, usually getting them wrong. The strangers bow stiffly. They shake hands like men clutching a
dead fish. One says, "Charmed," though he is bored. The other says, "Delighted," though he wishes to be elsewhere. They
stand there, paralyzed by the sudden intimacy, searching for something to say that is not about the weather. They usually
fail. The boy reckons that it would be more honest to simply sniff each other like dogs and get it
over with, as that method establishes the social standing much quicker and without the need for lying.

17. The Sacred Guest Towel In the bathroom hangs a towel of such pristine whiteness, embroidered with flowers, that it looks
like a holy relic. This is the Guest Towel. It is strictly for show. No member of the family dares
to touch it with a wet hand. Even the guest, if he has any breeding, knows it is a trap.
He looks at it, admires it, and then dries his hands on his own trousers or a piece of toilet
paper. The towel hangs there for years, unsoiled and useless, a symbol of the family's aspirations to elegance. The boy
learns that there are two types of objects in a house: those you use, and those you own merely to
prove you are civilized enough not to use them.

18. The Burden of "Good Breeding" "Good Breeding" is a term used to describe a person who can suffer inconvenience without
making a noise. If a gentleman is served a cup of coffee that is scalding hot, he must drink it
with a smile, burning the lining off his throat, rather than spit it out. If he sits on a tack,
he must rise slowly and with dignity. The natural man yells when he is hurt; the well-bred man pretends he
has no nervous system. The boy watches the adults at a dinner party, enduring tight shoes and boring conversation with
frozen smiles, and decides that "breeding" is just a fancy word for the ability to act like a statue while
being tortured.

19. The Cultural Duty Once a winter, the town feels obligated to attend a "Lecture" or a "Recital" to improve its
mind. They gather in a drafty hall to hear a man speak on "The Flora of the Andes" or to
listen to a violinist who saws wood for two hours. The seats are hard. The air is stale. Half the
audience falls asleep, but they do it sitting bolt upright, their eyes glazed over. They applaud wildly at the end,
not because it was good, but because it is over. They walk home telling each other how "edifying" it was,
while secretly promising themselves that next time, they will stay home and read the almanac.

20. The Etiquette of the Sneeze Nature designed the sneeze as an explosion, a violent clearing of the head. Society, however,
demands that it be suppressed. A man feels the tickle. He panics. He buries his face in a handkerchief. He
makes a strangled noise like a dying mouse. His eyes water; his ears pop. He apologizes to the room as
if he had committed a crime. To sneeze loudly is considered the mark of a boor; to suppress it is
the mark of a gentleman. The boy wonders why a natural bodily function is treated as a social disgrace, and
why we are expected to apologize for having a nose that occasionally works

21. The Fiction of "Not at Home" There is a polite fiction maintained by the upper crust known as being "Not
at Home." A visitor knocks. The servant opens the door and declares, with a face of stone, that the Mistress
is "Not at Home." Both the visitor and the servant know this is a lie; the Mistress is upstairs eating
a pickle and reading a novel. The visitor leaves a card and departs, relieved. The Mistress stays in her wrapper,
relieved. It is a conspiracy of deception that pleases everyone. The boy marvels at a moral code that considers it
a sin to tell a lie about a cherry tree, but a virtue to tell a lie about one’s whereabouts
to avoid a conversation with Mrs. Gable.

22. The Curse of the "Sunday Best" God made the dirt, and He made the boy, and the two were designed
to interact. But Society invented the "Sunday Best"—a suit of clothes made of fabric that attracts dust like a magnet.
The moment a boy is buttoned into this armor, he becomes a target. If there is a mud puddle within
a mile, he will fall into it. If there is a berry bush, it will stain him. He walks to
church in a state of terror, walking like a man on ice. He learns that "holiness" in the eyes of
his mother is not about the state of his soul, but the state of his knees, and that a spot
of grease on a lapel is a darker sin than a lie on the tongue.

23. The Etiquette of the Ugly Baby When a woman presents her new infant to the town—a red, wrinkled, screaming lump
of humanity—Society demands a specific lie. The observer must clasp their hands and cry, "What a beautiful child!" To speak
the truth—to say, "He looks like a boiled crab"—would be to declare war. The boy watches his mother coo over
a baby that resembles a gargoyle. He listens to her tell the father, "He has your nose," which is true,
and unfortunate. He realizes that the first rule of civilization is that all babies are beautiful, even the ones that
look like they were made in a hurry out of leftover dough.

24. The Tyranny of the Umbrella The umbrella is the only object in civilized society that is considered communal property. A
man who would never steal a dollar will steal an umbrella without a twitch of conscience. He takes a new
silk one from the rack at the church and leaves a broken cotton one in its place. He calls it
"an exchange." The next man takes the cotton one and leaves a frame of bare wires. The boy observes this
cycle of larceny and concludes that the umbrella is a test of character that no one passes, and that the
only way to keep a good umbrella is to never take it out when it rains.

25. The Boredom of the "At Home" The "At Home" is a social gathering where people stand around a room holding
teacups and balancing cake on their knees, terrified of dropping crumbs on the carpet. The conversation is a desert of
dullness. They discuss the weather. They discuss the roads. They discuss the health of people they don't know. The boy,
forced to hand around the sugar bowl, watches the guests. Their eyes are glazed. Their smiles are fixed. They look
like people waiting for a train that is late. He wonders why they don't just go home and be comfortable,
but he learns that in Society, being bored in a crowd is considered "entertainment."

26. The Law of the Pew A church pew is technically the House of God, but practically the private property of
the family that rents it. If a stranger wanders in and sits in the wrong pew, the owner stops at
the aisle and stares. He does not speak. He simply radiates outrage. He looks at the stranger, then at the
pew, then at the usher. The stranger, withered by this silent heat, shuffles away to the back. The owner sits
down, adjusts his cushion, and prays for humility, having just demonstrated that while salvation may be free, a good seat
near the pulpit is a matter of real estate and squatters' rights.

27. The Burden of the Wedding Gift When a couple announces their intention to marry, the community levies a tax in
the form of Silver. Friends and relations who have not spoken in years send pickle forks, butter knives, and soup
ladles. The young couple receives fourteen pie servers and not a single pie. They display these useless objects on a
table for the town to inspect. The donors come to check if their gift is in a prominent place. The
boy reckons that a young couple starting out would prefer a ham or a barrel of flour, but Society dictates
that they must starve off silver platters, surrounded by enough cutlery to equip a regiment.

28. The Pretense of the "Classic" There are certain books that everyone claims to have read and loved, but which no
one can actually finish without falling into a coma. The town intellectual carries a copy of Paradise Lost. He quotes
it. He praises its majesty. But the boy notices that the bookmark never moves past page ten. The book is
a prop. It exists to be seen on the table, signaling that the owner is a Man of Letters. The
boy learns that a "Classic" is a book that everyone wants to have read, but nobody wants to read, serving
chiefly as a decorative brick for the parlor table.

29. The Dance Card Calculus A ball is not a celebration; it is a market. The young ladies line up with
their cards, waiting for the young men to bid for a waltz. The popular girl’s card is full before the
music starts; she is the monopoly. The wallflower stands alone, studying her fan with intense interest, pretending she is merely
resting. The young men calculate their moves—dancing with the ugly cousin to please the rich aunt, avoiding the clumsy girl
who steps on toes. The boy watches this grim arithmetic and decides that a dance is just a business transaction
set to music, where hearts are broken and corns are stepped on in equal measure.

30. The "Good Morning" Mandate Society insists that the first greeting of the day must be "Good Morning," delivered with a
smile. This is required even if it is raining, the coffee is cold, and the house is on fire. A
man who comes down to breakfast and grunts is considered a bear. He must chirp. He must inquire about how
everyone slept, even if he heard them snoring through the wall. The boy finds this forced cheerfulness to be the
hardest work of the day. He believes that for the first hour after rising, the human race should be allowed
to hate each other in silence until the coffee takes effect.

31. The Mystery of the "Company Voice" The boy observes that his mother possesses two distinct voices. One is for the
family—a practical, flat tone used for ordering coal and correcting behavior. The other is the "Company Voice." This voice is
pitched an octave higher. It is musical, breathless, and full of strange vowels. When the Minister calls, the mother suddenly
speaks as if she were an actress in a tragedy. She laughs a tinkling, artificial laugh at jokes that are
not funny. The boy watches this transformation with awe. He wonders where this other woman comes from, and why she
disappears the moment the front door closes, replaced instantly by the woman who wants to know who left the muddy
boots in the hall.

32. The Ritual of the Carving Knife At a dinner party, the host is expected to carve the roast. This is
the supreme test of manhood. He sharpens the knife with a steel, making a clashing sound like a sword fight.
He eyes the bird like a gladiator. The guests watch in silence. He plunges the fork. He saws. He sweats.
The bird slides across the platter; gravy splashes onto the tablecloth. The host smiles grimly, pretending this is part of
the plan. He hacks off a piece of meat that looks like a wood chip. The guests accept it with
reverence. The boy realizes that the purpose of the ritual is not to divide the meat, but to humiliate the
father in his own home.

33. The Tyranny of the Napkin The napkin is a square of linen designed to confuse the uninitiated. There is a
great debate: does one tuck it into the collar, like a bib? Or lay it across the knees? If a
man tucks it in, he is protecting his vest but risking his reputation as a gentleman. If he lays it
on his lap, he preserves his dignity but ruins his trousers when the soup spills. The boy watches the guests
peering at each other, waiting for the hostess to make the first move. It is a game of "Follow the
Leader" played with fabric. He decides that the only safe place for a napkin is in the pocket, where it
can be used for its God-given purpose—wiping the nose.

34. The Etiquette of the Sick Room When a neighbor falls ill, Society demands a specific performance. One must visit the
house, walking on tiptoes and whispering. The visitor enters the sick room with a face of doom, even if the
patient only has a cold. They sit by the bed and recount stories of people who died from the exact
same symptoms. They offer remedies involving onions and flannel. They sigh heavily. They leave the patient exhausted and terrified. The
boy reckons that more people are killed by the sympathy of their neighbors than by the disease itself, and that
the best cure for any ailment is a locked door and a loaded shotgun to keep the comforters away.

35. The Law of the Borrowed Book To lend a book is a social act; to return it is a miracle.
A man borrows a volume of history, swearing on his honor to return it in a week. He takes it
home. He reads the first chapter. Then he uses it to press a leaf. Then he uses it to prop
up a table leg. The book becomes a permanent resident of his house. The owner sees it there when he
visits but is too polite to ask for it. The borrower forgets it is not his. The boy learns that
the surest way to lose a library is to have friends who can read, and that a book, once lent,
is gone forever, like a breath of wind.

36. The Pretension of the Menu The town hotel prints a menu for Sunday dinner. It is written in a language
that resembles French but is actually just bad spelling. They list "Pommes de Terre" which turns out to be mashed
potatoes with lumps. They list "Boeuf au Jus" which is a tough steak swimming in grease. The diners order these
dishes with an air of sophistication, pointing to the words they cannot pronounce. They eat the same food they eat
at home, but they pay double for the privilege of needing a translation. The boy concludes that a fancy name
is the best seasoning for a bad meal, as it gives the stomach something to think about while it struggles
with the digestion.

37. The "Long Goodbye" The departure of guests is a process that defies the laws of time. They say "We must
go" at eight o'clock. They stand up. Then they stand in the parlor for twenty minutes, talking about the very
things they discussed while sitting down. They move to the hall. They put on their coats. They talk for another
ten minutes with their hands on the doorknob. They open the door, letting in the cold air, and talk on
the porch. The host shivers and smiles, praying for their exit. The boy watches this agonizing retreat and wonders why
people who were in such a hurry to leave are so determined to stay in the doorway.

38. The Barbershop Parliament The Barbershop is the male equivalent of the Sewing Circle, but with more pomade. Here, men gather
to be shaved and to settle the affairs of the nation. The Barber is the Speaker of the House. He
holds a razor to a man's throat and gives his opinion on the tariff. The customer, unable to nod or
shake his head for fear of being cut, is forced to agree. They discuss the Sheriff’s new horse and the
Widow’s new bonnet. It is a place where every man is a philosopher, provided his mouth is not full of
lather. The boy listens and learns that the wisdom of the town is usually dispensed along with the bay rum.

39. The Fiction of the "Surprise Party" A "Surprise Party" is a conspiracy to invade a man’s home when he is
least prepared. The guests gather in the dark, giggling. They burst in, shouting. The victim is usually found in his
suspenders, reading the paper. He must feign delight. He must smile while strangers track mud onto his carpet and eat
his pantry bare. He spends the evening trying to look pleased while calculating the cost of the lamp they broke.
The boy observes that the only true surprise is that the host does not throw them all out, proving that
politeness is powerful enough to override the natural instinct for home defense.

40. The Definition of a "Lady" The town distinguishes sharply between a "Woman" and a "Lady." A Woman cooks, scrubs, and
raises children. A Lady does these things too, but she pretends she doesn't. A Lady never sweats; she "glows." A
Lady never eats heartily; she "pecks." A Lady faints when she sees a mouse. The boy watches the butcher’s wife,
a sturdy woman who can lift a side of beef, put on her Sunday gloves and suddenly become helpless. He
realizes that being a Lady is largely a matter of acting weaker than you are, a performance designed to make
men feel strong by comparison to a fainting spell.

41. The Tyranny of the Autograph Album Every young lady of breeding keeps a book bound in plush velvet, and she
demands that every visitor deface it with a sentiment. The victim is handed a pen and forced to be witty
on demand. He sits there, sweating, while the lady watches. He cannot think of a single rhyme. He ends up
writing "Roses are red, violets are blue," a sentiment so original it has appeared on every page since the book
was bought. He signs his name with a flourish to hide his shame. The boy concludes that the Autograph Album
is a device invented to prove that while many of us can write, very few of us have anything to
say.

42. The "Engagement" Fiction When two young people decide to marry, Society insists on a period called the "Engagement." During this
time, they are treated as invalids. They are whispered about. They are not allowed to be alone for fear they
might actually get to know each other. They sit in the parlor with the door open, while the mother knits
in the hall like a warden. They look at the floor. They look at the ceiling. They are bored to
distraction. The boy reckons that if couples were allowed to talk freely before the wedding, there would be fewer weddings,
which is exactly why Society forbids it.

43. The Despotism of the Clean Plate "Think of the starving children in China," the mother says, pointing to the boy’s
plate of boiled turnips. The boy tries to visualize how his eating a cold turnip in Missouri will assist a
child in Peking. The logic escapes him. He suggests mailing the turnip to China. This is considered insolence. He is
forced to swallow the vegetable as a moral duty. He learns that in our society, gluttony is a sin, but
wasting food is a crime, so the virtuous path is to overeat until you are sick, thereby satisfying both the
cook and the conscience.

44. The Etiquette of the Street There is a complex code governing how men walk on a sidewalk. A gentleman must
always walk on the outside, near the curb, to protect the lady from... well, no one is quite sure what.
Runaway horses? Mud splashes? Or perhaps falling masonry? He constantly shifts position as they turn corners, dancing around her like
a nervous spaniel. If he meets another man, they perform a do-si-do to avoid collision. The boy watches this pedestrian
ballet and decides that it would be simpler if everyone just walked in the middle of the street with the
horses, where at least the rules of collision are straightforward.

45. The Vanity of the Portrait Painter A traveling artist comes to town, promising to capture "The Real You" for five
dollars. The citizens line up. The result is always the same: a picture of a person who looks like they
have been stuffed with sawdust. The eyes follow you around the room, but not in a friendly way. The mouth
is a grim line. Yet, the family hangs it over the mantelpiece and claims it is a "speaking likeness." The
boy looks at the portrait of his uncle, which looks like a potato with whiskers, and marvels at the power
of art to make us look worse than we are, and charge us for the insult.

46. The Social Lie of "I'm Fine" Two men meet. One looks like he has slept in a haymow; his eyes
are red, his hand shakes. "How are you?" asks the first. "I am fine," says the wreck. It is the
Great American Lie. To admit misery is a breach of etiquette. A man could be standing in the middle of
his burning house, and if a neighbor asked, "How are you?" he would instinctively reply, "Can't complain." The boy learns
that conversation is not about exchanging facts, but about maintaining a smooth surface, and that the only acceptable answer to
"How do you do?" is a falsehood.

47. The Burden of the Family Bible In the center of the parlor table lies the Family Bible. It is the
size of a trunk. It has brass clasps. It is never opened, except to record a birth or a death.
It gathers dust. It is too heavy to read comfortably; if you put it on your lap, it cuts off
the circulation. It serves as a paperweight for the family's morality. The boy regards it with awe, not for its
contents, but for its sheer physical mass. He suspects that the family believes the size of the book compensates for
the smallness of their piety, and that a twenty-pound Bible makes them twice as holy as a ten-pound one.

48. The Tragedy of the "Spare Room" The house is small. The children sleep three to a bed. Yet, there is
a room upstairs that is always closed. The blinds are drawn. The furniture is shrouded in sheets. This is the
"Spare Room." It is kept for the mythological guest who never comes. It smells of lavender and loneliness. The boy
sneaks in sometimes and sits on the forbidden chair, feeling like a trespasser in his own home. He wonders at
the logic of a society that prioritizes the comfort of an imaginary stranger over the comfort of its own offspring.

49. The Ritual of the Tea Party The ladies gather at four o'clock to drink a hot liquid that none of
them particularly like. They hold the cups with their little fingers extended, a posture that suggests a cramp. They nibble
on biscuits that are dry as dust. The conversation is a minefield. One wrong word about the Parson's wife, and
the room freezes. They sip; they smile; they judge. The boy watches from the doorway, hoping for a leftover cake,
and decides that a Tea Party is a battlefield where the weapons are sugar tongs and the wounds are inflicted
by silence.

50. The Hypocrisy of the "Self-Made" Man The town boasts of its "Self-Made" millionaire. He struts. He claims he owes nothing
to anyone. But the boy remembers. He remembers the teacher who taught the man to read. He remembers the uncle
who lent him the first fifty dollars. He remembers the wife who darned his socks and cooked his meals while
he plotted his fortune. The man stands on top of a pyramid of other people's labor and claims he flew
there. The boy realizes that "Self-Made" is a term used by men who have bad memories and good imaginations.

Batch 6: The Final Absurdity 51. The New Year’s Penance Society has invented a torture called the "New Year’s Call."
On the first day of the year, a man is expected to visit every house he has neglected for the
previous twelve months. He puts on his tightest boots. He trudges through the snow. He enters the parlor, drinks a
thimble of punch, eats a stale cake, and says, "Happy New Year" to people he hopes not to see until
next January. He repeats this forty times. By evening, he is exhausted, his feet are frozen, and his stomach is
in rebellion. He crawls into bed, comforting himself that he has satisfied the demands of friendship without the inconvenience of
actually being a friend.

52. The Cult of the Ancestor There are families in town who live entirely in the past. They do not own
a good horse or a watertight roof, but they own a Grandfather who was a Colonel. They polish his reputation
daily. If a neighbor suggests that the Colonel was actually a bit of a drinker, the family bristles. They treat
the dead ancestor as a living deity. The boy notices that the more useless the present generation is, the more
they talk about the virtues of the dead one. He concludes that a family tree is like a potato plant—the
best part of it is usually underground.

53. The Social Standing of the Dog The dog is the only member of society who is allowed to be honest.
If he likes a man, he wags his tail. If he dislikes him, he bites his leg. He does not
smile at people he hates. He does not attend tea parties. Yet, he is welcomed everywhere. The boy observes that
the dog violates every rule of etiquette—he eats with his mouth open, he scratches in public, he sleeps in the
parlor—yet he is loved more than the Deacon. It proves that deep down, civilized man envies the savage, and that
we keep dogs because they represent the freedom we have traded for a set of silver forks.

54. The Pretension of the Opera A traveling Opera troupe comes to the hall. The town turns out in force. They
sit on wooden benches for three hours listening to a fat lady scream in Italian. No one understands a word.
The heat is stifling. The music sounds like a cat fight in a boiler factory. Yet, the audience sits with
rapt expressions, nodding in time to the noise. They applaud when the fat lady falls down. They walk home convincing
themselves they have been "cultured." The boy reckons that if you locked a man in a room and made him
listen to that noise, he would confess to murder just to make it stop.

55. The Shame of the Patch A boy tears his trousers. His mother patches them with a piece of cloth that
is a different color. To the boy, this is a tragedy. He feels the eyes of the world on that
square of fabric. He walks sideways to hide it. He refuses to turn his back on the girls. He learns
that in our society, it is better to have a hole in your character than a hole in your pants.
A man can be a liar, a cheat, and a scoundrel, and society will forgive him; but let him appear
in a coat with a patch on the elbow, and he is a pariah.

56. The Hair Brooch Horror There is a fashion among the ladies to wear jewelry made from the hair of the
dead. A widow wears a brooch containing a woven lock of her late husband’s hair. It rests on her bosom,
a macabre nest. The boy finds this terrifying. He imagines the ghost of the husband looking for his scalp. Yet,
society calls it "sentimental." The same people who scream at a spider will pin a piece of a corpse to
their dress and call it fashion. The boy decides that the line between affection and ghoulishness is thinner than a
hair, and that he prefers his dead ancestors to stay entirely in the graveyard.

57. The Cracker Barrel Congress The General Store is the true seat of government. The men sit around the stove, chewing
tobacco and whittling. They decide who is a good man and who is a fool. Their verdict is final. If
the Cracker Barrel Congress decides a man is "stuck up," he is ruined. If they decide he is "good folks,"
he can borrow money anywhere. They operate without written laws, judging a man by the way he treats his horse
and the way he takes a joke. The boy listens from behind the counter and learns that the true laws
of society are not written in books, but spoken in spit and sawdust.

58. The Tyranny of the "Nice" The highest compliment a mother can pay a boy is to say he is "Nice."
To a boy, this is an insult. "Nice" means he is clean; "Nice" means he does not fight; "Nice" means
he is essentially a girl. He wants to be "Brave," or "Wild," or "Dangerous." But society values the Nice. It
rewards the boy who sits still and says nothing. The boy realizes that "Nice" is simply a word adults use
for a child who has successfully been crushed into submission, and he resolves to be as "Un-Nice" as possible for
as long as he can get away with it.

59. The Fiction of the "Happy Family" Every family presents a united front to the world. They walk to church in
a phalanx. They smile. They agree. But the boy knows the truth. He knows about the argument over the burnt
toast. He knows about the silence at the dinner table. He knows his sister hates his brother. Yet, the moment
a neighbor appears, the masks slide into place. They become the Ideal Family. The boy marvels at this collective acting
performance. He wonders if every house on the street is a theater where the curtains are always drawn, and the
actors are only honest when the audience is gone.

60. The Torture of the Corset The boy watches his sister dress for a ball. She takes a deep breath and
grabs the bedpost. Her mother pulls the strings of the corset until the girl’s ribs crack. She turns purple. She
gasps. "Pull tighter," she says, "I must have a waist." She reduces her middle to the size of a napkin
ring. She cannot eat; she cannot breathe; she can barely walk. And she calls this "Beauty." The boy thinks of
the greyhound, who is thin by nature and can run like the wind, and compares it to his sister, who
has made herself a cripple for the sake of a silhouette.

61. The Equality of the Dentist’s Chair There is one place where all social distinctions vanish: the Dentist’s chair. The Banker
sits there, trembling. The Judge sits there, weeping. The Bully sits there, pale as a sheet. When the Dentist produces
the forceps, the man with a million dollars is no better off than the man with a dime. They both
open their mouths and surrender. The boy, who fears the Dentist more than death, takes a grim satisfaction in knowing
that the high and mighty are just as cowardly as he is when faced with a pair of pliers, proving
that a toothache is the great leveler of mankind.

62. The Final surrender The boy grows up. He swore he would never wear a stiff collar; he wears one now.
He swore he would never make a New Year’s Call; he made ten yesterday. He swore he would never lie
about the weather; he does it every morning. He has become a Citizen. He sits in the parlor, worrying about
what the neighbors think. He has traded his raft for a mortgage and his freedom for a reputation. He looks
out the window at a ragged boy running down the street, barefoot and dirty, and he feels a pang of
envy so sharp it feels like grief. He realizes he has joined the conspiracy, and the trap has closed at
last.
